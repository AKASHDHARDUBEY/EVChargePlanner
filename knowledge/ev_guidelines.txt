# EV Charging Infrastructure Planning Guidelines

## 1. General Principles
- Prioritize equitable access by installing charging stations in disadvantaged communities, adhering to the federal Justice40 Initiative.
- Adopt "EV-ready" building codes and streamline permitting processes to reduce installation costs and installation complexity.
- Engage local electric utilities early in the planning process. This prevents unexpected capacity limits and allows for managed upgrade costs.

## 2. Station Placement Strategy
- **Corridor-Level**: Focus on highways and interregional travel routes. Fast chargers (DCFC) should be placed every 50 miles along major corridors.
- **Community-Level**: Focus on regional transit centers, public parking lots, and multi-unit dwellings. Destination charging (Level 2) is highly effective where vehicles remain parked for >2 hours.
- **Site-Level**: Ensure installations meet ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act) accessibility standards. Consider the physical footprint, lighting, and clear EV-only signage.

## 3. Load Management & Schedulling
- Implement Time-of-Use (TOU) pricing algorithms in planning assumptions to encourage off-peak overnight charging.
- Use staggered charging schedules for fleets (like delivery trucks or school buses) to avoid exceeding transformer capacities.
- Integrate smart chargers that can dynamically adjust output power based on the building or grid’s overall load curve.

## 4. Grid Integration Strategies
- Pair EV charging stations with local solar photovoltaic (PV) generation and battery energy storage systems (BESS) where the grid is constrained.
- Utilize load balancing features provided by Networked charging software to prevent drawing more power than the site can support.
- Assess historical utility demand data. Sites with significant spare electrical capacity generally make the best rapid-deployment targets.
